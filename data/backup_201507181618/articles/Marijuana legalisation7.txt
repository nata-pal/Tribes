<p>CLEVELAND&#8211; Ohio is one step closer to legalized <a href="http://fox8.com/tag/marijuana/" target="_blank">marijuana</a>. The group pushing to get the issue on the ballot, &#8220;ResponsibleOhio,&#8221; has begun canvassing for signatures in Ohio’s biggest cities, including Cleveland, days after the Ohio Ballot Board gave the green light to begin doing so.</p> <p>ResponsibleOhio, which has raised millions of dollars to support its effort, said it started gathering signatures outside Cleveland City Hall and the Cuyahoga County Justice Center this week. Staff and volunteers are expected to be at other locations and events in the coming months. Supporters must collect more than 305,000 signatures by July for Ohio voters to decide on the legalization of marijuana in November.</p> <p>“We&#8217;re just starting,” ResponsibleOhio spokesperson Lydia Bolander said. “It&#8217;s only been a couple days we&#8217;ve been out collecting, so there&#8217;s a long road ahead, but we&#8217;re really excited to be getting underway with this.”</p> <p>The proposed amendment would legalize the sale of marijuana for both medicinal and recreational use in the state of Ohio for adults over age 21 and create authorized growing sites across the state.</p> <p>Supporters tout the potential economic and tax impacts.</p> <p>“We all understand that marijuana is in our communities now, but rather than being something that is safe and tightly regulated, it&#8217;s something that actually promotes crime in our communities,” Bolander said. “So, we need to tax it; we need to regulate it; we need to legalize it.”</p> <p>Opponents call the proposal dangerous.</p> <p>“The impact on society is what concerns me and primarily our youth,” said Derek Siegle, Executive Director of the Ohio High Intensity Drug Trafficking Area, a federal program that funds local drug task forces. Siegle pointed to negative impacts of Colorado’s law allowing recreational use of marijuana and said he worries about addiction.</p> <p>“It impairs your judgment. It impairs your motivation. There&#8217;s been workplace issues with it,” he said. “I think there’s a whole host of things that can go wrong, and I just think we need to slow down and instead of looking at what everybody wants for themselves, look at what’s the good and the impact on society.”</p> <p>If voters pass the amendment, Ohio would become the fifth state in the nation to allow recreational use of marijuana.</p>
