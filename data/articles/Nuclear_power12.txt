href='https://www.blogger.com/post-edit.g?blogID=8803908253839878060&postID=5735903139112267105&from=pencil' title='Edit Post'> <img alt='' class='icon-action' height='18' itemprop='articleBody'> <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on"> <div dir="ltr" style="text-align: left;" trbidi="on"> SUBHEAD: Obama's final Clean Power Plan drops support for existing nuclear plants.  <br /> <br /> <span style="font-weight: bold;"> By Jeff McMahhon on 3 August 2015 for Forbes - </span><span style="font-size: small;">  </span> <br /> (<a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jeffmcmahon/2015/08/03/final-clean-power-plan-drops-support-for-existing-nuclear-plants/">http://www.forbes.com/sites/jeffmcmahon/2015/08/03/final-clean-power-plan-drops-support-for-existing-nuclear-plants/</a>)  <br /> <br /> <img border="0" src="http://www.islandbreath.org/2015Year/08/150807indian.jpg" />  <br /> <span style="font-size: small;">Image above: </span><span style="font-size: small;">The aging Indian Point Nuclear Power Plant on the banks of the Hudson River upstream from NYC. Photo by Don Emmert for AFP/Getty Images. From original article.</span><br /> <br /> The final version of President Obama&#8217;s Clean Power Plan does not  include&nbsp;aid to existing nuclear power plants at risk of closing because  they&nbsp;can&#8217;t compete&nbsp;with cheaper natural gas and renewables&#8212;a list that  includes some of the nation&#8217;s most controversial reactors, including  Indian Point and Three Mile Island.<br /> <br /> In the draft version, EPA had proposed allowing states&nbsp;to count 6  percent of existing nuclear generation toward their clean energy goals, a  provision designed to rescue&nbsp;the 6 percent of nuclear capacity  considered at risk.<br /> <br /> &#8220;On further consideration, we believe it is inappropriate to base the  BSER (Best System of Emission Reduction) on elements that will not  reduce CO2 emissions from affected electric generating units below  current levels,&#8221; EPA <a href="http://www.epa.gov/airquality/cpp/cpp-final-rule.pdf" target="_blank">states</a> in the final rule.<br /> <br /> &#8220;Existing nuclear generation helps make existing CO2 emissions lower  than they would otherwise be, but will not further lower CO2 emissions  below current levels. Accordingly&#8230;&nbsp;the EPA is not finalizing  preservation of generation from existing nuclear capacity as a component  of the BSER.&#8221;<br /> <br />Accordingly,&nbsp;<span class="tweet_quote"> <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1P1CnPJ&amp;text=States%20will%20not%20be%20able%20to%20meet%20their%20clean%20energy%20goals%20by%20extending%20the%20licenses%20of%20existing%20%23nuclear%C2%A0plants.">states will not be able to meet their clean energy goals by extending the licenses of existing nuclear&nbsp;plants.</a></span><br /> <br /> The revised rule does have some good news for nuclear utilities:  <br /> <ul style="text-align: left;"> <li>&nbsp;<span class="tweet_quote"><a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1P1CnPJ&amp;text=EPA%20will%20allow%20Southern%20states%20to%20count%205.5MW%20of%20electricity%20from%20new%20reactors%20under%20construction">EPA will allow Southern states to count 5.5GW of electricity from new reactors under construction</a></span>  &nbsp;in South Carolina, Georgia and Tennessee toward their&nbsp;goals. The draft  rule had counted that power as existing, rather than new generation.  EPA encourages states to include &#8220;the continued commitment of the  owner/operators to completion of the new units&#8221; in their plans. The  owner operators are&nbsp;SCANA Corp in South Carolina, Southern Company in  Georgia, and the Tennessee Valley Authority.<br /> <br /> </li> <li>And <span class="tweet_quote"> <a href="https://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=http%3A%2F%2Fonforb.es%2F1P1CnPJ&amp;text=EPA%20will%20allow%20states%20to%20count%20uprates%20at%20nuclear%20plants">EPA will allow states to count uprates at nuclear plants</a></span>  . Uprates allow reactors to produce more thermal energy than their  initial approval,&nbsp;sometimes&nbsp;through the use of richer uranium&nbsp;fuel,  taking advantage of overcapacity built into the plants.</li> </ul> </div> Older nuclear plants can&nbsp;only help states meet their&nbsp;goals  though&nbsp;uprates that would have to be approved by the Nuclear Regulatory  Commission during&nbsp;license extensions.<br /> Quite a few at-risk reactors are operated by Exelon Corp, owner of  the nation&#8217;s largest nuclear fleet, which has been lobbying for renewed  government support. When I contacted him this afternoon, Exelon  spokesman Paul Elsberg&nbsp;said the company had not had sufficient time to  review the final version of the&nbsp;Clean Power Plan to comment:<br /> <blockquote class="tr_bq"> <i>&#8220;It&#8217;s obviously something Exelon is examining closely Exelon has long supported efforts to reduce carbon and other  dangerous air emissions from the power sector while maintaining reliable  and affordable energy for our customers. We are evaluating EPA&#8217;s final  rule and look forward to continuing to engage with EPA, states and all  stakeholders on the most cost effective means to achieve targeted  greenhouse gas reductions.&#8221;</i></blockquote> Entergy, owner of the Indian Point  Energy Center&nbsp;near New York City and two other reactors considered at  risk said this:<i> </i><br /> <blockquote class="tr_bq"> <i><i>&#8220;The U.S. EPA&#8217;s final Clean Power Plan is complex, and it will  take time for Entergy to conduct its full analysis,&#8221; Entergy said in a  prepared statement sent by spokeswoman Kay Jones. The statement says  Entergy continues to be concerned about he legality of EPA&#8217;s approach,  and it&#8217;s analysis will include&nbsp;&#8221;the impact on the nation&#8217;s existing  nuclear fleet, which in 2014 comprised nearly 61 percent of U.S.  emissions-free generation.&#8221;</i> </i></blockquote> In 2013, Morningstar <a class="exit_trigger_set" href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/jeffmcmahon/2013/11/07/6-nuclear-plants-that-may-be-next-to-shut-down/" target="_blank">identified</a> six nuclear plants that could be next to shut down because of economic conditions:<br /> <ol style="text-align: left;"> <li><b>Indian Point</b>, NY, owned by Entergy</li> <li><b>Ginna</b>, NY, Exelon</li> <li><b>Fitzpatrick</b>, NY, Entergy</li> <li><b>Three Mile Island</b>, PA, Exelon</li> <li><b>Davis Beese</b>, OH, FirstEnergy</li> <li><b>Pilgrim</b>, MA, Entergy</li> </ol> Exelon has also <a href="http://www.chicagobusiness.com/article/20140301/ISSUE01/303019987/exelon-warns-state-it-may-close-3-nukes" target="_blank">identified</a> its&nbsp;Byron, Quad Cities, and Clinton plants in Illinois as at risk and has campaigned against tax credits for wind energy.<br /> <br /> The nuclear industry had campaigned for stronger support for nuclear  power in the Clean Power Plan.<br /> <br /> American Nuclear Society President&nbsp;Brady  Raap <a href="http://www.nuclearconnect.org/issues-policy/epa_clean_power_plan" target="_blank">argued</a>  that the draft&nbsp;rule&nbsp;&#8221;almost completely discounts the clean energy  contributions of current nuclear energy facilities, while penalizing  states that have new plants under construction.&#8221;<br /> <br /> ANS took it as a <a href="http://www.ans.org/pi/news/article-434/" target="_blank">good sign</a> when EPA Administrator Gina McCarthy said EPA would reconsider the rule&#8217;s treatment of nuclear.<br /> <br /> <br /></div> <div style='clear: both;'></div> </div> <div class='post-footer'> <div class='post-footer-line post-footer-line-1'> <span
