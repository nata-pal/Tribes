.entry-header -->  	<div class="entry-content"> 		<h1><em>Introduction: the genetic manipulation referred to in this article is *not* about crossing different breeds of sheep, developing various hybrids of corn, or other &#8220;within species&#8221; enhancement to improve naturally-occuring traits. </em></h1> <h1><em>Instead it&#8217;s about using a bacteria or virus to artificially insert an entirely foreign DNA into a plant&#8217;s genes, such as <a href="http://www.worldwatch.org/node/4956" target="_blank">human genes inserted into rice</a>, or Monsanto&#8217;s &#8220;Bt&#8221; (Bacillus thuringiensis) corn, which was genetically modified to produce a protein that <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/news/2011-12-06/monsanto-corn-may-be-failing-to-kill-bugs-in-4-states-epa-says.html" target="_blank">ruptures the stomach</a> when ingested by pests.</em></h1> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/3d_model_dna_w_phosphate_1.jpg"><img class="alignleft  wp-image-369" style="margin-right:15px;" title="3d_model_DNA_w_phosphate_1" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/3d_model_dna_w_phosphate_1.jpg?w=270&#038;h=270" alt="" width="270" height="270" /></a> The first risk of genetic engineering is the very theory underlying its science and methods, which was brought to light by the United States National Human Genome Research Institute.</p> <p>All along, genetic modification has been based on a theory that one gene will express (or &#8220;code for&#8221;) one protein, and thus an organism&#8217;s genome  its total complement of DNA genes  should fully account for its unique pattern of inherited traits.</p> <p>However, when the Human Genome Research Project was completed in 2002, it proved <a href="http://www.artsci.wustl.edu/~anthro/articles/Commoner%20UNRAVELING%20THE%20DNA%20MYTH%20Feb02.htm" target="_blank">this theory was not true</a>.</p> <h1>The Human Genome Research Project discovered that genes operate in a complex network in ways that are still not fully understood.</h1> <p>The human genome has just under 25,000 genes, yet our bodies function with approximately 100,000 proteins. This is not a one-to-one ratio.</p> <p>There are far too few human genes to account for the <a href="http://www.actionbioscience.org/genomic/carroll_ciaffa.html" target="_blank">complexity of our inherited traits</a>, not to mention the vast inherited differences between plants  including the unrelated genes of the bacteria or viruses with which plants are being genetically manipulated.</p> <p>&#8220;A genome is a complex ecosystem that is greatly influenced by the environmenteach gene of a genome makes many proteins according to environmental cues,&#8221; <a href="http://www.vancouversun.com/health/Genetically+modified+food+rapped/8308415/story.html" target="_blank">Dr. Thierry Vrain</a>, former research scientist for Agriculture Canada.</p> <p><strong>DNA contains a newly-discovered &#8220;second level&#8221; of code</strong></p> <p>In a <a href="http://www.washington.edu/news/2013/12/12/scientists-discover-double-meaning-in-genetic-code/" target="_blank">December 2013 study</a>, scientists at the University of Washington discovered a <em><strong>second code</strong></em> hiding inside DNA, which completely changes how scientists read the instructions contained in DNA, and how they interpret mutations to make sense of health and disease.</p> <p style="color:#373737;">According to the lead scientist (Genome scientist Dr. John Stamatoyannopoulos at the University of Washington, pictured above), The fact that the genetic code can <em><strong style="font-style:inherit;">simultaneously write two kinds of information</strong></em> means that many DNA changes that appear to alter protein sequences may actually cause disease by disrupting gene control programs or even both mechanisms simultaneously.  Dec. 13 issue of <i>Science.</i></p> <p><strong>Gene Modification is Not Specific, Precise, or Predictable</strong></p> <p>Because we don&#8217;t entirely understand how genes workas evidenced both by the Human Genome Project&#8217;s findings as well as the recent discovery of a second string of code inside DNAthe very process of genetic engineeringthe <a href="http://www.fas.org/biosecurity/education/dualuse-agriculture/2.-agricultural-biotechnology/methods-of-gene-transfer-in-plants.html" target="_blank">random insertion of a gene</a> into the genomecauses disruptions in many enzymes that perform basic metabolic work.</p> <p><a href="http://www.vancouversun.com/health/Genetically+modified+food+rapped/8308415/story.html" target="_blank">Says Dr. Vrain,</a> &#8220;Every scientist now learns that any gene can give more than one protein and that inserting a gene anywhere in a plant eventually creates rogue proteins. Some of these proteins are obviously allergenic or toxic.&#8221;</p> <p>To put it simply, the Human Genome Research Project completely undermined the &#8220;science&#8221; behind genetic engineering. The whole paradigm of genetic engineering technology was based on a misunderstanding. The &#8220;Big Ag&#8221; corporations&#8217; claims about their methods of genetically modifying food crops being &#8220;specific, precise, and predictable&#8221; are <a href="http://thegeneticengineeringdebate.blogspot.ca/2012/10/the-anti-sciene-of-genetic-engineering.html" target="_blank">entirely untrue</a>.</p> <h2>Modifying one segment of DNA does not have a single direct result; instead it can cause a spiraling effect of unintended consequences</h2> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/junk_food.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-370 alignleft" style="margin-right:15px;" title="junk_food" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/junk_food.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></p> <p>Long before the Human Genome Research Project&#8217;s findings, <a href="http://www.realfoodexplained.com/hidden-dangers-of-the-gmo-technology/" target="_blank">a study published in 1999</a> in the <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2706426/" target="_blank">International Journal of Biological Sciences</a> found that inserting a gene into another organism&#8217;s DNA 1) causes thousands of activations, not just the one trait the researcher is looking for, 2) activates non-targeted (and sometimes toxic) genes; 3) affects idle genes, with entirely unknown effects; 4) lowers the plant&#8217;s nutrient content (since the plant&#8217;s energy is consumed with producing unnecessary proteins activated by the insertion).</p> <p>Due in no small part to those findings, a group of scientists wrote a now-landmark letter titled &#8220;<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10524614">Beyond Substantial Equivalence</a>&#8221; to the prestigious journal <em>Nature</em>. In the letter, Erik Millstone<em> et. al.</em> called the FDA on the carpet in regard to their having branded genetically modified foods as &#8220;substantially equivalent.&#8221; Mr. Millstone called this term a &#8220;pseudo-scientific concept&#8221; that is &#8220;inherently anti-scientific because it was created primarily to provide an excuse for not requiring biochemical or toxicological tests.&#8221;</p> <p>Fast forward to this decade, and the results of the Human Genome Research Project are once again being proven: while working to find allergens in their GMO crops, scientists discovered that the genetic regulatory sequence used in more than 60% of GMO crops encodes a significant fragment of a &#8220;viral&#8221; gene that, in their words, might result in unintended phenotypic changes.&#8221;</p> <p>During this same study, they also discovered some GMO crops had &#8220;superfluous&#8221; and &#8220;unsuspected&#8221; genes, including incomplete or rearranged sequences. <a href="http://independentsciencenews.org/commentaries/regulators-discover-a-hidden-viral-gene-in-commercial-gmo-crops/" target="_blank">The results of this study</a> could not more clearly underscore what the Human Genome Research Project has been saying all along:</p> <h2><em>&#8220;Genetic engineering is an experiment in the proposition that human institutions can perform adequate risk assessments on lab-created living organisms&#8221;</em></h2> <p>As Barry Commoner, American biologist, college professor, author, and former Editor of Science Illustrated, says, &#8220;The most dramatic achievement to date of the $3 billion Human Genome Project is the refutation of its own scientific rationale,&#8221; as <a href="http://harpers.org/archive/2002/02/unraveling-the-dna-myth/" target="_blank">excerpted from an article in Harper&#8217;s Magazine.</a></p> <p>If the Human Genome Research Project clearly demonstrated the inherent flaws of genetically engineered agriculture, why has this corollary been swept under the media carpet in the United States?</p> <p>Barry Commoner summarizes it like this in that same article: &#8220;biotechnology companies are not in the habit of publicizing studies that question the efficacy of their miraculous products.&#8221;</p> <p>Quite the opposite in fact:  these &#8220;ag-bio&#8221; corporations are in business to <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/01/06/business/monsanto-logs-126-million-profit-topping-expectations.html" target="_blank">make a profit</a>, with their primary allegiance to shareholders. They craft a strategic wall of corporate protection using <a href="http://www.infowars.com/extensive-list-of-politicians-paid-off-by-monsanto/" target="_blank">paid politicians</a> who will <a href="http://www.motherjones.com/tom-philpott/2013/04/sen-roy-blunt-monsantos-man-washington" target="_blank">write laws in their favor</a>, with <a href="http://www.earthisland.org/journal/index.php/elist/eListRead/major_universities_come_out_in_support_of_monsanto_in_supreme_court_case/" target="_blank">paid &#8220;research&#8221;</a> that is conducted to <a href="http://www.alternet.org/story/155375/how_corporations_like_monsanto_have_hijacked_higher_education" target="_blank">show the presumed safety</a> of their products, with ex-employees who they&#8217;ve assisted in gaining <a href="http://gmo-awareness.com/all-about-gmos/gmo-fda/" target="_blank">seats in the FDA and USDA</a>, and with <a href="http://www.foodsafetynews.com/2013/05/center-for-food-safety-lists-food-industry-front-groups/#.UbeDaRYz3TQ" target="_blank">food industry front groups</a> and public relations firms that are paid to counter real research.</p> <p>So what are the inherent risks of consuming food that contains <a href="http://www.alternet.org/story/150733/why_is_damning_new_evidence_about_monsanto%27s_most_widely_used_herbicide_being_silenced/" target="_blank">unexpected genetic behavior</a>? What are the <a href="http://www.latimes.com/business/la-fi-gmo-mystery-20110402,0,3650400.story" target="_blank">resulting outcomes</a> to the crops and the environment?</p> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/dead-monarch-butterflies.jpg"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-372" style="cursor:default;border:0 initial initial;" title="ARKive image GES055739 - Monarch butterfly" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/dead-monarch-butterflies.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></p> <h1><strong>The Risks of Genetic Engineering</strong></h1> <p>The outward risks of genetically engineered foods fall into three categories:</p> <p style="padding-left:30px;">1.  Environmental Hazards<br /> 2.  Human Health Risks<br /> 3.  Economic Concerns</p> <h2><strong>I.    ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS</strong></h2> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Unintended harm to other organisms</span></strong></p> <p>Over a decade ago, a laboratory study published in <span style="text-decoration:underline;"><a href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v399/n6733/full/399214a0.html" target="_blank">Nature</a></span><sup><a href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v399/n6733/full/399214a0.html" target="_blank">1</a></sup> showed that pollen from corn that was genetically modified to produce its own insecticide caused high mortality rates in monarch butterfly caterpillars (article abstract <span style="text-decoration:underline;"><a href="http://www.pages.drexel.edu/~ls39/peer_review/losey1.htm" target="_blank">here</a></span>); the data was corroborated in a more recent study, as cited in a <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/07/12/science/12butterfly.html" target="_blank">July 2011 article in the NY Times</a>, and once again in a study by the University of Minnesota, as published online in the journal Insect Conservation and Diversity in <a href="http://www.startribune.com/local/143017765.html" target="_blank">March 2012</a>.</p> <p>But monarchs are suffering on another level: crops that are genetically engineered to resist weedkillers and the resulting increased use of weedkillers have <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/news/technology/monarch-butterfly-decline-linked-to-spread-of-gm-crops-1.2665131" target="_blank">all but exterminated milkweed</a>the <em>only</em> plant used by Monarch butterflies to lay their eggs, and Monarch caterpillars to eat. As of January 2014, the Monarch population is at its <a href="http://www1.umn.edu/news/news-releases/2014/UR_CONTENT_469850.html" target="_blank">lowest point in history</a>.</p> <p>And it&#8217;s not just butterflies&#8230; experts believe the dramatically decreasing populations of bees both in the United States and abroad are directly <a href="http://www.spiegel.de/international/world/0,1518,473166,00.html" target="_blank">related to genetically engineered plants</a>, their pollen, and <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25063858" target="_blank">recent studies</a> also indicate negative honeybee impacts from the herbicide Glyphosatethe key ingredient in RoundUp, which Monsanto&#8217;s GMO crops are engineered to withstand.</p> <p><a href="https://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/05/roundup-ready-crops.png"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-956" src="https://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/05/roundup-ready-crops.png?w=584&#038;h=139" alt="roundup-ready-crops" width="584" height="139" /></a></p> <p><a href="http://www.non-gmoreport.com/articles/nov07/gm_corn.php">Another study</a> back in 2007 showed that the corn&#8217;s insecticidal toxins leached into nearby streams, causing increased mortality and reduced growth of caddisflies, an aquatic insect related to the pests targeted by the toxin in GMO corn (yes, this problem has been going on that long!). Further, because caddisflies are a food resource for fish and amphibians, contamination spreads further when these insects are consumed by its natural predators. First reported on the <a href="http://newsinfo.iu.edu/news/page/normal/6507.html" target="_blank">Indiana University</a> website, the study was subsequently published in the <em><a href="http://www.pnas.org/content/104/41/16204.abstract" target="_blank">Proceedings of the National Academies of Science</a></em>; read the abstract (with associated commentaries) <a href="http://lawprofessors.typepad.com/foodlaw/2007/10/ge-corn-may-har.html" target="_blank">here</a>.</p> <p>It is not possible to design a B.t. toxin to be genetically engineered into corn that would only kill crop-damaging pests while remaining harmless to all other insects.</p> <p>&#8220;It&#8217;s like AIDS,&#8221; says Michael McNeill &#8212; an agronomist who received his Ph.D. in quantitative genetics and plant pathology from Iowa State University in 1969 and has been a crop consultant since 1983. He was among three experts invited by county officials to testify at the August 10, 2011 meeting of the Cropland Policy Advisory Group (CPAG).  <a href="http://www.boulderweekly.com/article-6211-expert-gmos-to-blame-for-problems-in-plants-animals.html" target="_blank">Read his summary here</a>.</p> <p><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/canola-weeds.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-373 alignnone" style="margin-right:15px;" title="canola weeds" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/canola-weeds.jpg?w=360&#038;h=270" alt="" width="360" height="270" /></a></span></p> <p><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><strong>Reduced effectiveness and increased use of pesticides</strong></span></p> <p>Just as some populations of mosquitoes developed resistance to the now-banned Monsanto insecticide DDT, insects are <a href="http://www.monsanto.com/newsviews/Pages/india-pink-bollworm.aspx" target="_blank">quickly becoming resistant</a> to crops that have been genetically modified by Monsanto to produce their own insecticides.</p> <p>Back in 2008, the USDA was <a href="http://www.ars.usda.gov/research/publications/publications.htm?seq_no_115=234995" target="_blank">already finding higher damage rates</a> / insect survival rates than a study they performed just two years prior. In 2011,  increasing damage due to corn <a href="http://spectrumseed.com/Rootworm%20Resistance%20to%20Bt%20Corn.pdf" target="_blank">rootworm evolution</a> was being reported, as cited in a study by <a href="http://spectrumseed.com/Rootworm%20Resistance%20to%20Bt%20Corn.pdf" target="_blank">Aaron J. Gassman</a>, and also <a href="http://www.agriview.com/news/crop/western-corn-rootworm-resistance-turns-up-in-iowa/article_d0d74504-cf2f-11e0-ad2a-001cc4c002e0.html" target="_blank">reported by several midwest universities</a>; followed by <a href="http://www.bloomberg.com/news/2012-11-16/dupont-dow-corn-defeated-by-armyworms-in-florida-study.html" target="_blank">armyworm evolution</a> in late 2012.</p> <p>The most recent study found <a href="http://grist.org/news/five-pest-species-now-immune-to-gmo-corn-and-cotton/" target="_blank">more than 1/3 of the 13 major pest species</a> have become immune to GMO corn and cotton, and several others were in the process of developing resistance. In the <a href="http://www.nature.com/nbt/journal/v31/n6/full/nbt.2597.html" target="_blank">scientist&#8217;s</a> own words, &#8220;You&#8217;re always expecting the pest to adapt. It&#8217;s almost a given that preventing the evolution of resistance is not possible.&#8221;</p> <p>The same is true of herbicides: genetically modified canola (engineered to withstand Round-Up) is now <a href="http://www.non-gmoreport.com/articles/may2011/gmcanolaherbicideresistantweed.php" target="_blank">spreading as an uncontrollable, invasive weed</a> in California.</p> <p>And it&#8217;s not just the crops that are spreading. More and more varieties of &#8220;superweeds&#8221; are becoming <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/05/04/business/energy-environment/04weed.html" target="_blank">increasingly resistant</a> to any known weedkillers, while <a href="http://www.fastcompany.com/1768090/resistant-weeds-take-roots-and-threaten-food-supply" target="_blank">driving up the cost of food</a> in direct proportion.</p> <p><a href="http://www.stltoday.com/business/local/article_f01139be-ace0-502b-944a-0c534b70511c.html" target="_blank">Back in July 2011</a>, the superweeds were becoming so powerful that farmers were being forced to use older, more toxic chemical sprays, more frequently and in heavier volumes, or spend extra money hiring day laborers to literally chop out the plants&#8230; some of which were reported to have stems as thick as 4 inches in diameter, growing three inches a day, and <a href="http://www.fastcompany.com/1768090/resistant-weeds-take-roots-and-threaten-food-supply" target="_blank">damaging conventional farm machinery</a>.</p> <p>As of May 2012, farmers interviewed by <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/05/04/business/energy-environment/04weed.html?pagewanted=all" target="_blank">the New York Times</a>  reported that weed control was &#8220;back to where we were 20 years ago.&#8221;</p> <p>No surprise Monsanto&#8217;s <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2012/01/06/business/monsanto-logs-126-million-profit-topping-expectations.html?_r=0" target="_blank">profits exceeded expectations</a> in 2012  after being paid for the privilege to purchase their &#8220;RoundUp Ready&#8221; seeds each year, farmers must pay them even more for ever-increasing volumes of RoundUp.</p> <p>Experts are calling the superweed epidemic &#8220;the single largest threat to production agriculture that we have ever seen,&#8221; warning that it could lead to higher food prices, lower crop yields, rising farm costs, and even greater pollution of land and water.</p> <p>Biotechnology&#8217;s response: <a href="http://newsroom.dowagro.com/press-release/enlist-e3-soybean-brand-announced" target="_blank">Dow Agrosciences introduced</a> a soybean that has been genetically engineered to resist three herbicides: glyphosate, glufosinate, and a 2-4,D formula (one of the main ingredients of Agent Orange) &#8212; enabling farmers to use three levels of weed control.</p> <p>In summary, one of the greatest promises of the agri-bio industry &#8212; that GMO crops would reduce the use of chemicals &#8212; is sadly untrue: pesticide use has <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2012/10/02/us-usa-study-pesticides-idUSBRE89100X20121002" target="_blank">increased by 404 million pounds</a> from the time genetically engineered crops were introduced back in 1996, to the year 2011. <a href="http://www.co.lake.ca.us/Assets/BOS/GE+Crops+Committee/Gen+Eng+Crops+and+Pest+Use+in+the+U.S..pdf" target="_blank">View the original report here</a>; view the EPA&#8217;s <a href="http://www.epa.gov/oppfead1/cb/csb_page/updates/2011/sales-usage06-07.html" target="_blank">older report on pesticides here </a>(they have not published an updated report since 2006-2007). Considering how   <a href="http://www.epa.gov/pesticides/food/risks.htm" target="_blank">detrimental pesticides are</a> to human health&#8230; (you can read more about that <a href="http://www.nbcnews.com/id/44260583/ns/health-childrens_health/t/pesticides-food-linked-adhd-kids/#.UYGsMpUz3TQ" target="_blank">here</a>).</p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Uncontrolled biological pollution</span></strong></p> <p><a href="http://www.centerforfoodsafety.org/issues/311/ge-foods/ge-food-and-the-environment" target="_blank">The Center for Food Safety (CFS)</a> states the environmental <a href="http://userwww.sfsu.edu/~rone/GEessays/nocontainment.htm" target="_blank">impacts of GMOs</a> will include an &#8220;uncontrolled biological pollution, threatening numerous microbial, plant and animal species with extinction, and a potential contamination of all non-genetically engineered life forms with novel and possibly hazardous genetic material.&#8221; Read more <a href="http://www.ebr-journal.org/action/displayAbstract?fromPage=online&amp;aid=8240065&amp;fulltextType=RA&amp;fileId=S163579221100100X" target="_blank">here</a>, and <a href="http://www.newscientist.com/article/dn4709-crops-widely-contaminated-by-genetically-modified-dna.html" target="_blank">here</a>, and <a href="http://online.sfsu.edu/%7Erone/GEessays/FrankencropsPollution.html" target="_blank">here</a>.</p> <p>GMO Canola is <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?feature=player_embedded&amp;v=jEX654gN3c4" target="_blank">spreading via migratory birds</a>, who eat the plants in one area, then fly and deliver &#8220;fertilized seeds&#8221; to the fields of another area. Monsanto&#8217;s telling farmers they must pull these weeds by hand, since none of Monsanto&#8217;s weed sprays are killing their own spray-resistant plants.</p> <p>Another study published in the Journal of Bioscience in June 2011 showed that long-term effects of genetic engineering is in the plant itself: resulting in <a href="http://www.telegraphindia.com/1110603/jsp/nation/story_14065280.jsp" target="_blank">developmental defects, growth retardation and sterility</a>.</p> <p><a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2010/04/13/us-usa-gmos-regulators-idUSTRE63C2AJ20100413" target="_blank">Fifteen years of research by the USDA</a> indicates that the chemical glyphosate, the key ingredient in RoundUp herbicide which is genetically engineered into, and heavily sprayed onto all GMO crops, could be causing fungal root disease resulting in detrimental impacts to the root structure of plants, said Bob Kremer, a microbiologist with the U.S. Department of Agriculture&#8217;s Agricultural Research Service.</p> <p>Studies have also shown that <a href="http://www.non-gmoreport.com/articles/jan10/scientists_find_negative_impacts_of_GM_crops.php" target="_blank">soil biology</a> is negatively impacted when it&#8217;s used to grow GMO crops, as reported in the October 2009 issue of <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/journal/11610301/31/3" target="_blank">The European Journal of Agronomy</a>.</p> <p>And once it&#8217;s in the soil, it gets into our water. The increased use of herbicides (including older, more toxic chemical &#8220;cocktails&#8221; to combat superweeds) is leaching through the soil <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22101424" target="_blank">into the groundwater supplies (NIH study from March 2012)</a> beneath &#8220;America&#8217;s breadbasket,&#8221; where it&#8217;s drawn back up as <a href="http://www.latimes.com/news/local/la-me-seville-water-20110425,0,1938488.story" target="_blank">drinking water</a> and crop irrigation via wells.</p> <p>Even before the NIH study, in 2011 three separate universities <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2011/08/31/us-glyphosate-pollution-idUSTRE77U61720110831" target="_blank">and the USGS</a> found that RoundUp (glyphosate) is present in both the water, <em>and the air</em>, in significant levels. Glyphosate, the key ingredient in &#8220;Roundup&#8221; herbicide, was found <a href="https://gsa.confex.com/gsa/2009AM/finalprogram/abstract_162346.htm" target="_blank">in <em>every</em> stream sample</a> examined in Mississippi over a two-year period (fishing, anyone?), and also detected in Iowa streams, as well as most air samples taken (breathing, anyone?).</p> <p>Need a complete list of glyphosate studies with sources? <a href="http://gmo-awareness.com/resources/glyphosate/" target="_blank">Click here.</a></p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Gene transfer to non-target species</span></strong></p> <p>Another concern is the natural cross-breeding of crops in adjacent fields, resulting in the <a href="http://www.jstor.org/discover/10.2307/30034113?uid=3739960&amp;uid=2&amp;uid=4&amp;uid=3739256&amp;sid=21101959350203" target="_blank">transfer of transgenes</a> into organic and conventional (non-genetically-engineered) crops.</p> <p>Sadly, farmers who become the victim of natural crop <a href="http://responsibletechnology.org/cap-the-gene-spill" target="_blank">cross over</a> are often subject to lawsuits: Monsanto has repeatedly filed patent infringement lawsuits against farmers who may have inadvertently harvested GM crops mixed into their non-GM crops. Farmers insist these crops are the result of cross-pollination from GM crops planted a field or two away; Monsanto claims the farmers obtained Monsanto-licensed GM seeds from an unknown source, and mixed them into their seed sources without paying royalties to Monsanto.</p> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/soybean-field-and-farmer.jpg"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-375" title="Soybean-Field-and-Farmer" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/soybean-field-and-farmer.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></p> <p>Monsanto came up with a solution for cross-breeding: <a href="http://www.banterminator.org/The-Issues/Introduction" target="_blank">terminator seeds</a>. These seeds were genetically engineered to only survive for one season, thus preventing cross-contamination from one season to the next (unless the plants cross-pollinate neighboring non-GM plants prior to harvest), and forcing farmers to purchase seeds from Monsanto every year, rather than following the age-old practice of saving seeds from one harvest to plant the next. (Though their contract already stipulates that they must buy new seeds each year, this approach was thought to guarantee it.) Fortunately, Terminator Seeds have not yet been brought to market.</p> <p>Another solution posed by Monsanto are buffer zones to inhibit cross-over by GM crops<sup>5, 6, 7</sup>. By planting a buffer of non-GM, non-harvestable corn around a field of GM corn, they suggest that beneficial or harmless insects would have a refuge in the non-GM corn and could destroy the non-GM corn without develop resistance to the GM corn&#8217;s inherent pesticides (provided of course that the insects understand which zone is which, and restrict their activities to the non-GM zone&#8230;&#8230;.).</p> <p>Monsanto further postulates that buffer zones ranging from 6 to 30 meters or more<sup>8</sup> would likewise inhibit gene transfer to weeds and other crops because the wind-blown pollen would not travel beyond the buffer zone (provided there are no strong winds or tornadoes).</p> <p>However buffer zones would still not address genetically engineered plants being transferred by migratory birds eating the seeds. And to date, few farmers have been willing to lose valuable production yields to let land go fallow in non-usable buffer zones.</p> <h2>2.   HUMAN HEALTH RISKS</h2> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/gmo_corn_mexico.jpg"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-377" title="gmo_corn_mexico" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/gmo_corn_mexico.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Emerging Health Risks in Human Studies</span></strong></p> <p>Nearly two decades after genetic engineered crops have been in your grocery store, human studies are only now starting to be performed &#8212; <em>but not in the United States.</em></p> <p>Why is no research being done on the potential human health risks of GMOs in our country? Scientists must literally ask these corporations for permission before publishing independent research on genetically modified crops. (<a href="http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=do-seed-companies-control-gm-crop-research" target="_blank">Source: Scientific American Magazine, August 13, 2009</a>)</p> <p>Not surprisingly, no such permission has been granted in the last twenty years, except for studies that are crafted to put GMOs in a positive context.</p> <p>Two years later, the scientific journal <span style="text-decoration:underline;">Science Direct</span> published a study in April 2011 in which they found <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/B6VCB-51S6FY9-1/2/08d7c6d3dab66367125cf64e3140e87c" target="_blank">significant conflict of interest</a> in research done on the health risks and nutritional assessment of genetically modified products. &#8220;Where there was a conflict of interest, 100% of the studies (41 out of 41) made a favorable GM safety finding.&#8221;</p> <p>As recently as 2014, a comprehensive peer review of the Ag-Biotech industry&#8217;s so called &#8216;long term safety studies&#8217; Researchers at the University of Caen (France) showed that toxins produced in, for example, the genetically engineered maize MON810, can significantly impact the viability of human cells.&#8221;</p> <p>As stated previously, the use of pesticides has <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2012/10/02/us-usa-study-pesticides-idUSBRE89100X20121002" target="_blank">increased dramatically</a> since the advent of genetically engineered crops. GMOs represent a double chemical dose delivered directly to your dinner tableboth inside and outside of the plant. (Or triple, if you count sprayed chemicals leaching into soil and groundwater&#8230;).</p> <p>Outside of the U.S., a <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/health/article-1388888/GM-food-toxins-blood-93-unborn-babies.html" target="_blank">February 2011 study</a> linked the insecticidal toxin found in genetically modified crops to the blood of pregnant women, their fetuses, and non-pregnant women in Canada. View the <a href="http://www.usherbrooke.ca/gnec/pj/Article%20paru%20dans%20Reproductive%20Toxicology%20(document%20PDF).pdf" target="_blank">original study document here</a> (online viewable pdf).</p> <p>But it&#8217;s not just about the toxins we&#8217;re ingesting&#8230;</p> <p><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><strong>Documented Proof of Modified Genes Surviving the Digestion Process</strong></span></p> <p>New studies are disproving one of the biggest assurances that pro-GMO manufacturers and scientists continue to make: that &#8220;new genes introduced in GM food are harmless, since all genes are broken up and rendered inert during digestion.&#8221;</p> <p>The first study done in the U.K. indicates a potential release of genetically altered DNA in human digestive tracts: &#8220;the possibility of functional DNA release from plant GMOs cannot be excluded. It showed that ingested plant microRNA &#8212; such as the genetically modified bits containing Bt &#8212; not only survive digestion, but <em>most definitely influence human cell function.</em> This means that DNA can code for microRNA, which can, in fact, be hazardous&#8230; having been linked for ten years to human diseases including cancer, Alzheimer&#8217;s, and diabetes. What a contrast  to Monsanto&#8217;s claim that there is no need for, or value in testing the safety of GMO foods in humans. Read the summary article <a href="http://www.alternet.org/story/153737/how_genetically_modified_foods_could_affect_our_health_in_unexpected_ways/" target="_blank">here</a>; view the original study report <a href="http://www.nature.com/cr/journal/v22/n1/full/cr2011158a.html" target="_blank">here</a>.</p> <p>A third study in Norway, <a href="http://sciencenordic.com/growing-fatter-gm-diet" target="_blank">published in July 2012</a>, proved that GMO genes are indeed transferred through the intestinal wall into the blood. During their study they found &#8220;pieces of genetically modified DNA in large enough segments to be identified in blood, muscle tissue and liver.&#8221;</p> <p>Not only did that Norwegian study once again disprove the long-held &#8220;pro-GMO&#8221; claim that &#8220;new genes introduced in GM food are harmless since all genes are broken up in the intestines,&#8221; the test animals also showed increased weight gain, increased appetite, decreased immune function, an inability to properly digest proteins, as well as a different intestinal microstructure. In this study, they found that the double stranded RNA (dsRNAs) present in genetically engineered wheat were able to withstand digestion (even after cooking) and circulate through the body, where it amplified into more and different dsRNAs and &#8220;alters gene expression in the animal.&#8221; The scientist went on to state: &#8220;The molecules created in this wheat, intended to silence wheat genes, can match human genes, and through ingestion, these molecules can enter human beings and potentially silence our genes. The findings are absolutely assured. There is no doubt that these matches exist.&#8221; Read a synopsis article <a href="http://www.digitaljournal.com/article/332822" target="_blank">here</a>; view the full study report <a href="http://safefoodfoundation.org/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/Heinemann-Expert-Scientific-Opinion.pdf" target="_blank">here</a>.</p> <p>So what if these altered genes aren&#8217;t digesting, what&#8217;s the inherent risk?  These studies indicate that the food we eat transfers more than just vitamins and protein to our cells. Our bodies are <a href="http://www.theatlantic.com/health/archive/2012/01/the-very-real-danger-of-genetically-modified-foods/251051/" target="_blank">absorbing information, aka microRNA</a>. What genes would you like to have &#8220;turned down or turned off&#8221; in your body, without your knowledge or permission?</p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;"><a href="http://www.responsibletechnology.org/rats-study-russian-press-release" target="_blank"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-376" style="margin-right:15px;" title="GMO rat study" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/gmo-rat-study.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></span></strong></p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Documented Health Risks in Animal Studies</span></strong></p> <p>Long before any studies were done in humans, countless animal studies<sup>9,10,11,12,13,14,15,16 </sup> indicate serious health risks associated with GMO food consumption.</p> <p>The association of GM foods and specific disease processes is also supported. No longer possible&#8230; that&#8217;s considered illegal patent infringement in Monsanto&#8217;s contract.</p> <p>Farmers are required to buy fresh seed every single year, and <a href="http://www.ethicalinvesting.com/monsanto/news/10040.htm" target="_blank">new laws</a> against &#8220;seed cleaning&#8221; businesses are causing these service providers to <a href="http://aidindia.org/main/content/view/758/355/" target="_blank">go out of business</a> &#8212; but not before Monsanto obtains their account records in order to track down farmers who are still cleaning and saving seeds.</p> <p>And if Monsanto seeds happen to contaminate a neighboring farmer&#8217;s field? The farmer pays for clean up. It&#8217;s <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/eric-holt-gimenez/not-monsantos-fault-ever_b_827924.html" target="_blank">not Monsanto&#8217;s fault. Ever.</a></p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Corporate Patents Control Nations</span></strong></p> <p>The higher cost of genetically modified &#8220;super seeds&#8221; is typically out of the range of what small farmers and third world countries are able to afford, thus widening the gap between wealthy and poor, well-fed and hungry.</p> <p>Unless of course Monsanto, the Rockefeller Foundation, and Bill and Melinda Gates step in to &#8220;gift&#8221; GMO seeds to poor nations (via non-profit organization called <a href="http://www.salem-news.com/articles/april012011/gm-seeds-india.php" target="_blank">AGRA</a>) in order to establish that country&#8217;s dependence on Monsanto&#8217;s &#8220;drug&#8221; and thus effectively take control of a poorer region&#8217;s economic future (not to mention the future of the population&#8217;s health).</p> <p><strong><span style="text-decoration:underline;">Lawsuits Force Non-GMO Farmers Out of Business</span></strong></p> <p>Farmers who don&#8217;t currently use Monsanto&#8217;s patented GMO seeds may face unexpected costs when their crops are cross-pollinated from neighboring GMO fields and Monsanto takes them to court for <a href="http://www.grist.org/sustainable-food/2011-03-31-reversing-roles-organic-farmers-sue-monsanto-over-gmo-seeds" target="_blank">patent infringement</a> (since Monsanto can&#8217;t sue the bees and butterflies actually doing the job).</p> <p>To date, only one farmer in <a href="http://www.percyschmeiser.com/" target="_blank">Canada</a> has been able to prevail against Monsanto in the lawsuit they filed against him. A class action lawsuit involving <a href="http://www.cornucopia.org/2011/05/family-farmers-amplify-legal-complaint-against-monsantos-gmos/" target="_blank">300,000 individuals, organizations, seed growers, and 4,500 farms</a> against Monsanto was dismissed in court in March of 2012, but an appeal is <a href="http://www.osgata.org/osgata-et-al-v-monsanto/" target="_blank">currently underway</a>. Click on <a href="http://www.cornucopia.org/2011/05/family-farmers-amplify-legal-complaint-against-monsantos-gmos/" target="_blank">this article</a> to see a complete list of plaintiffs at the bottom of the article.</p> <p>But farmers aren&#8217;t the only ones Monsanto sues. When Vermont and Connecticut tried to label GMOs via state legislative ballot, all Monsanto had to do was <a href="http://gmo-awareness.com/2012/08/21/the-war-against-gmo-labeling/" target="_blank">threaten a lawsuit</a> to get the ballots removed. Now California citizens have a labeling proposition going to voters in November 2012.</p> <p><a href="http://gmo-awareness.com/2012/08/21/the-war-against-gmo-labeling/">Monsanto is countering</a> by rallying nearly $50 million in anti-label advertising dollars, and that, combined with an ardent citizens&#8217; rebuttal, has created a surge of public awareness. If passed, Proposition 37 will be a monumental step forward in allowing consumers to make informed choices about food, while setting the stage for other states to follow California&#8217;s lead.</p> <p><a href="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/monsanto-anti-labeling-graph.jpg"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-848" title="Monsanto anti labeling graph" src="http://gmoawareness.files.wordpress.com/2011/04/monsanto-anti-labeling-graph.jpg?w=584" alt=""   /></a></p> <p>Ask yourself: why do the world&#8217;s top chemical manufacturers want to prevent you from knowing what&#8217;s in your food?
