And the world has responded with resounding anger and sadness. With nations <a href="http://www.enca.com/africa/botswana-bans-sports-hunters-after-cecil">publicly condemning</a> trophy hunting and the nation of Zimbabwe <a href="http://www.enca.com/africa/zimbabwe-calls-us-hunter-be-extradited-over-lion-death">calling for the extradition</a> of Palmer so he may answer for his crimes, it doesn<U+0092>t sound like the shock surrounding Cecil<U+0092>s demise is fizzling any time soon.</p> <p>Within that shock are a myriad of opinions, some of which clash, yet many decry the <U+0093>sport<U+0094> of trophy hunting. One particularly loud voice among the masses, at least in animal rights circles, is that of people who are pointing out the hypocrisy in the public becoming outraged at the needless death of one endangered animal, yet turning blind eyes to the billions of factory farmed animals who meet the same fate each year.</p> <p>As a fellow vegan, my heart similarly breaks for the careless killing of Cecil, as well as the deaths of each and every pig, cow, chicken, duck, rabbit and fish for human consumption. Yet, I have observed just as many inviting pleas for this recognition from veg folk as I have strong and combative words. Shockingly, I<U+0092>ve seen the same people who advocate for compassion wish for the death of Palmer, call out meat-eaters to be just as bad as Palmer, and referring to the hunter with terms that would likely get me flagged on some type of watch list for repeating.</p> <p>Militant vegan, treehugger, self-righteous&#8230;these are all terms most vegans have encountered from time to time from people unhappy with either the messages vegans deliver, or the format of those messages. While we cannot completely control how others perceive us or what we have to say, there certainly are ways to make discussion more inviting while simultaneously getting people to look more closely at everyday actions and their impact.</p> <p><strong>Empathy is Needed for Humans, Just as Much as Animals</strong></p> <p>While this perspective is often used as an excuse not to scrutinize animal product consumption (<U+0093>let<U+0092>s take care of our fellow humans, first!<U+0094>), there is a useful directive in there. As vegheads who once consumed animal products know, there was a time when we did not know<U+0097>or were hesitant to take a closer peek at<U+0097>the horrors of factory farming and the lives of farmed animals. Does that mean we were terrible monsters for continuing to eat meat, dairy and eggs? Of course not. Would we have appreciated the insinuation? Of course not!</p> <p>While tapping into empathy for people who choose to consume animals may be difficult, it is necessary so that there can be meaningful connection and conversation. What were your reactions to (publicly recognized) animal cruelty back then? Most likely shock, anger and outcry. These reactions are borne of compassion for the voiceless<U+0097>of which anybody and everybody is capable, vegan or not.</p> <p><strong>Talk From Your Heart</strong></p> <p>When stumped about how to assert one<U+0092>s voice on an important matter and not offend, falling back on the old fashioned <U+0093>I-statement<U+0094> is always an option, and a great one. Here<U+0092>s the formula:</p> <p><U+0093>I feel ___ when ___, because ___.<U+0094></p> <p>Fill in the emotion felt, the observable facts and the (non-judgmental, non-alienating) reason why. This format takes the focus off of the audience, as well as the expectation for the audience to suddenly change. It minimizes defensiveness and, truthfully, I-statements cannot be argued with, because they are identifying what the speaker feels and why.</p> <p>Using I-statements can start authentic, gear-turning conversations. For instance, saying <U+0093>I feel frustrated when I see people upset about Cecil<U+0092>s death and not the deaths of factory farmed animals, because I know the pain and suffering they endure is awful and happens on such a massive scale<U+0094> would probably be more well-received than <U+0093>You hypocrite, don<U+0092>t you see that eating your precious steak dinner is just as bad as trophy hunting?!<U+0094></p> <p><strong>Take Action</strong></p> <p>Anyone who is touched by others<U+0092> pain and hardship needs an appropriate outlet. Some suggestions are<U+00A0>signing and forwarding <a href="http://www.thepetitionsite.com/takeaction/610/392/435/">petitions</a>, becoming active in a local animal rights organization, coordinating opportunities for people to try out delicious vegan food (did someone say potluck?), or joining an online community of people<U+00A0>with the same ambition for advocacy.</p> <p>Also, journaling or confiding in someone who understands the more harsh emotions that arise from tragedies such as these is equally important. This way, difficult emotions can be adequately processed and restructured into inviting, non-reactive discussion. Find more <a href="http://www.joyfulvegan.com/blog/10-strategies-for-effective-communication-part-one">Strategies for Effective Communication</a> with omnivores on Colleen Patrick-Goudreau<U+0092>s podcast.</p> <p>What are some other ideas for<U+00A0>starting dialogue between omnivores and vegfolks?</p> <p><strong>Related<br /> </strong><a
