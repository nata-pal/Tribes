itemtype="http://schema.org/Article">  							 							 							<div class="text-content">  								 								<p><strong>KINGSTON, Jamaica, August 7, 2015 (AMG)</strong>&#8212;These days Jamaica is notoriously known as one the most homophobic places in the world, first identified as such in a Time magazine <a href="http://content.time.com/time/world/article/0,8599,1182991,00.html">article</a> in 2006. Yet in June of this year, the same magazine published an <a href="http://time.com/3900934/most-homophobic-place-on-earth-turning-around/">article</a> whose author questioned if “the most homophobic place on earth” was turning around. As of the past week, it seems that the tides are indeed changing favorably. Coinciding with the &#8220;Emancipendence period&#8221;, <a href="http://jflag.org/about/">J-FLAG</a> (Jamaican Forum for Lesbians, All-Sexuals, and Gays), Jamaica’s only organization promoting LGBT rights, successfully staged “The PRiDE of a People: Breaking the Rules of Oppression”, the country’s first ever LGBT pride celebrations, from August 1-5, 2015.</p> <p>The auspicious execution of PRiDE JA 2015 marks the fact that, even though progress is slower than desired, attitudes towards persons of LGBT communities and LGBT persons&#8217; perceptions of their place in Jamaican society are positively evolving. Contrary to popular belief, this is not solely due to the importation of “American gay culture” or external pressure from other countries or international human rights organizations. Rather, within Jamaica itself, increased visibility of LGBT persons, activism and advocacy for equal treatment, and the expression of support by influential Jamaicans are some of the internal factors that have assisted with the transition to a more progressive and inclusive society.<b></b></p> <p>Five days of well-planned events to celebrate the LGBT community and its allies took place during the period between Emancipation Day (August 1) and Independence Day (August 6). When asked why PRiDE celebrations were taking place during the week of independence, J-FLAG responded: “To celebrate our freedom, independence, and pride as LGBT Jamaicans”. According to Latoya Nugent, J-FLAG’s Education and Training Manager, the tremendous amount of local and global support and response via email, social media, participation, and patronage has been touching and inspiring. She also divulged that many people reached out to her directly, stating that the notion of LGBT pride celebrations in Jamaica was both startling and puzzling to them, and that most could not even conceive of it happening in the first place.</p> <p style="text-align: left;"><b>Anti-LGBT sentiments fueled by law</b></p> <p>Jamaica has a long history of anti-LGBT sentiments that would understandably make a Pride celebration seem unlikely. A common justification for negative feelings towards LGBT people is the nation’s anti-sodomy or “buggery” laws, which are vestiges of British colonial influence. The <a href="http://moj.gov.jm/sites/default/files/laws/Offences%20Against%20the%20Person%20Act_0.pdf">Offences Against the Person</a> act was imposed by Britain in 1864, and <a href="http://jflag.org/what-jamaican-law-says-about-homosexuality-buggery-law/">four of its articles</a> specifically make some reference to anal sex, described as the “abominable crime” or a “gross indecency”, which is subject to imprisonment.</p> <p>It is pivotal to note that being LGBT is not illegal in Jamaica&#8211; only the sexual behaviors thought to be “gay acts” are (although not all gay partners engage in anal sex and anal sex is not limited to only gay partners). As such, in addition to people’s personal, religious, or moral beliefs, much of the persecution of or violence towards LGBT people in Jamaica is buttressed by an antiquated law on the books. Speaking to this, Dane Lewis, the executive director of J-FLAG, stated:</p> <blockquote><p>it can be said that anti-sodomy laws serve to reinforce cultural hostility toward gay men and have also been known to embolden those with pre-existing prejudices. The anti-buggery law is often seen as representative of the moral position of the country with respect to the legitimacy or propriety of LGBT identity.<i>  </i></p></blockquote> <p><b>Jamaican attitudes towards same sex partnerships</b></p> <p>In order to explore this moral position and further understand people’s perceptions of same sex relations in Jamaica, researchers at the University of the West Indies, Mona conducted a <a href="http://jflag.org/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/2012-ATTITUDES-AND-PERCEPTIONS-OF-JAMAICANS-TOWARDS-SAME-SEX-RELATIONSHIPS.pdf">study</a> in 2012. They found that, in a nationally representative sample of 1000 Jamaicans, 88% felt that “male homosexuality” was immoral, 83.7% believed that female “homosexuality” was immoral, and 83.5% felt that bisexuality was immoral. They also found that 76.7% of those surveyed disagreed with changing the buggery law. Intriguingly, 37% of the survey participants admitted that they did not think the government was doing enough to protect the rights of members of LGBT communities in the country. The study also found that while negative views towards same sex relationships persist throughout Jamaican society, religious persons are becoming more tolerant, and young people have less negative views than older Jamaicans. These results were shown to be similar to a study done in 2011, with little variance between the two.</p> <p><b>“Not Safe at Home” report</b></p> <p>The fact that the laws (even if not officially or consistently enforced) have not changed and most Jamaicans have negative views towards the LGBT community demonstrates the precarious social environment in which LGBT people in Jamaica live, particularly if they publicly identify or present as LGBT.</p> <p>On October 21, 2014, <a href="https://www.hrw.org/">Human Rights Watch</a> (HRW), a nonprofit nongovernmental human rights organization, published “Not Safe at Home”, a <a href="https://www.hrw.org/report/2014/10/21/not-safe-home/violence-and-discrimination-against-lgbt-people-jamaica">report</a> on violence and discrimination against LGBT people in Jamaica. The report included the story of sixteen-year old Dwayne Jones, a transgender teen who was brutally murdered on July 21, 2013. Her death served as the catalyst for public debate within the country, having received national, regional, and international attention. Situating Dwayne’s story as a snapshot of how people in the LGBT community are sometimes treated in Jamaica, the report details human rights violations against LGBT people between 2006 and 2013 by both citizens and the state. It also includes findings from over 70 interviews (conducted in April and June 2014) of Jamaicans who identify as LGBT, “representatives of government agencies, United Nations (UN) officials, representatives of NGOs specializing in HIV/AIDS or human rights, academics, health care workers, and members of Jamaica’s police force.”</p> <p>This report appropriately called for accountability on the part of the Jamaican government. HRW made several recommendations, urging political officials to ”translate words into action and ensure the equal rights of all persons regardless of their gender identity or sexual orientation”.</p> <p><b>PRiDE JA of great importance to many</b></p> <p>Knowing this background, it is easy to see why PRiDE JA 2015 is so paramount. Jamaica’s planning of PRiDE celebrations comes after Suriname’s first Pride in 2011 (also CARICOM’s first Pride) and Curaçao’s first Gay Pride Week in 2012. Last year the Bahamas attempted to host their <a href="http://www.antillean.org/bahamas-first-gay-pride/">first Pride celebration</a>, called the Bahamas Pride Freedom Weekend, from Friday, August 29, 2014 to Tuesday, September 2, 2014. This would have been the first of such celebrations in the Anglophone Caribbean. However, on the first day, after organizer Victor Rollins received death threats when he posted photos on Facebook of participants holding rainbow flags with Bahamian flags, Rollins made the call to cancel the remaining events due to safety and security concerns.</p> <p>Maurice Tomlinson, one of the Caribbean’s leading gay rights activists, deftly explains the value of PRiDE JA especially for the Jamaican LGBT community:</p> <blockquote><p>Pride celebrations, no matter how small or clandestine, are incredibly important to build community and empower LGBTI people to claim their rights. In turn, an empowered LGBTI* community will be inspired to be more visible.  Research has shown that it is visibility that will undo homophobia. That is why PrideJA is so important.  It will encourage greater visibility of, and thus greater tolerance for, same-gender loving members of the Jamaican family. The fact that Jamaican LGBTI people feel confident enough to mount a Pride event certainly shows that there is greater acceptance by some elements of society, particularly members of the intelligentsia. Sadly, there is still a lot of work to do in changing the hearts of minds of the still largely fundamentalist population.</p></blockquote> <p>Indeed there is still quite a bit of work to be done. But J-FLAG&#8217;s efforts in producing this monumental celebration are commendable and motivational, and PRiDE JA 2015 undoubtedly signals the turning tides of moving from tolerance to acceptance.</p> <p><em>*LGBTI is an alternative acronym to the more common one of LGBT. One iteration of LGBTI stands for &#8220;Lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and intersex&#8221;. Acronyms to represent the diversity of sexual minorities come in many forms with multiple meanings. </em></p> <p><i>Image Credit: </i><i>J-FLAG</i></p> <p><em><strong>This is Part 1 of a three-part series on PRiDE JA 2015. Stay tuned for the Part 2, in which we interview Latoya Nugent for deeper insight into the planning and impact of PRiDE in Jamaica.</strong></em></p> <p>&nbsp;</p> <!-- Simple Share Buttons Plus
