class="entry-header"> 		<h1 class="entry-title">Lesson One, Section Four:  Measuring Humanism&#8217;s Modern Racism</h1>  		 	</header><!-- .entry-header -->  	<div class="entry-content post_content"> 		<p style="text-align: justify;">Effectively, by the end of the nineteenth century, the world had been racialized and humanism had been complicit, if not outright culpable, in helping this process to unfold.  In particular, humanism and science collided in various ways to support the racial order.  <b><i>Scientific racism</i></b>, the direct use of science to validate racist thinking such as differential worth among different races, became popular in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.  And it would have dastardly consequences. <a href="http://cohe.humanistinstitute.org/contemporary-topics/humanism-and-race/lesson-one-section-four-measuring-humanisms-modern-racism/attachment/220px-phrenologypix/" rel="attachment wp-att-8799"><img class="alignright size-full wp-image-8799" alt="220px-PhrenologyPix" src="http://cohe.humanistinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/220px-PhrenologyPix.jpg" width="220" height="274" /></a><b><i> Phrenology</i></b> is an example of this scientific racism.  Phrenology was a pseudo-science that sought to make concrete observations and measurements of human skulls and assumed that such measurements corresponded to the abilities and affective sensibilities of those measured.  As you might imagine, white men were treated as the norm, and blacks (and Jews) were measured so that older, racist stereotypes might be reinforced.  It was thought that these physical measurements could tell the scientist everything from a person’s IQ to a person’s propensity towards violence or laziness.<span id='easy-footnote-1' class='easy-footnote-margin-adjust'></span><span class='easy-footnote'><a href='#easy-footnote-bottom-1' title='For more on phrenology, in particular its social effects and the manner in which it relied on stacking the statistics in favor of the group assumed to superior, white men, see the well-known Stephen Jay Gould&#8217;s <em>The Mismeasure of Man</em> (Norton &amp; Co., 1996), 92.3, throughout.'><sup>1</sup></a></span><a title="" href="#_edn1"><sup><sup><br /> </sup></sup></a></p> <div> <p style="text-align: justify;">The predilection for drawing social conclusions about humans from physical features didn’t stop with the cranium, but the face, too.  In particular, racist stereotypes of Jews having big noses and blacks having big lips and hips were used popularly, as if the butt of a joke that never ended—a “joke” motivated by overarching preoccupations with human possibility vis-a-vis a white European ideal.  The effects were hardly funny; they reified racist and colonialist thinking, justifying the subjugation of Jews in Europe, blacks and native peoples in the Americas, and colonial conquest abroad.</p> <p style="text-align: justify;"><a href="http://cohe.humanistinstitute.org/contemporary-topics/humanism-and-race/lesson-one-section-four-measuring-humanisms-modern-racism/attachment/tibetexpediton-anthropometrische-untersuchungen/" is a humanism in that it rests on a determination of <i>humanitas </i>that it sees as more powerful, that is more effective, than any other.”’<span id='easy-footnote-2' class='easy-footnote-margin-adjust'></span><span class='easy-footnote'><a href='#easy-footnote-bottom-2' title='Tome Rockmore, <em>Heidegger and French Philosophy:  Humanism, Antihumanism, and Being, </em>(Routledge, 2002), 160.'><sup>2</sup></a></span>  Such claims may be overgeneralized and ring as ridiculous to contemporary humanists, but the point of such perspectives was often to demonstrate that the humanist preoccupation with metaphysics and its “answer” to metaphysics that posits “man as the measure of all things” was (and is) ostensibly <b><i>fascist</i></b>, in that it assumes the supreme value of one particular idea (represented by one particular type of person) and judges all other ideas (and people) against this arbitrary norm.</p> <p style="text-align: justify;">Though not everyone railed so vehemently against humanism, by the end of the second world war, most in Europe and the Americas were moving beyond the deep trust in human potential and possibility that marked science and humanism’s strange, deadly modern courtship.  Two particularly famous humanists in the post-war period were Jean-Paul Sartre and Frantz Fanon, the former a philosopher and the latter a psychologist and anticolonial freedom fighter.  Both men understood that any humanism moving forward had one issue to resolve above all else: the issue of racialized thinking, and the humanist tendency to rank social differences.  On the issue of fascism and responding to the war, Fanon suggested that fascism was a version of colonialism turned inward, and Jean-Paul Sartre called colonialism a boomerang, that would inevitably return to Europe.<span id='easy-footnote-3' class='easy-footnote-margin-adjust'></span><span class='easy-footnote'><a
