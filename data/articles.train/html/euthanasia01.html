Friday, August 7, 2015

Washington State annual report: Assisted suicide deaths continue to rise.

Alex Schadenberg
By Alex Schadenberg
Executive Director, Euthanasia Prevention Coalition

In 2014, 126 people reportedly died by assisted suicide in Washington State up by 6% from 119 assisted suicide deaths in 2013 representing a 43% increase from 83 assisted suicide deaths in 2012.

According to the annual report, there were 176 lethal prescriptions written resulting in 126 assisted suicide deaths, 17 deaths from other causes, 27 deaths from an unknown cause and 6 people remain alive.

The annual report states that they do not know if the 27 deaths from unknown causes died by assisted suicide. It is possible that some or all of these people died by assisted suicide.

In the Netherlands approximately 23% of the assisted deaths are not reported. In Belgium the percentage of unreported deaths is even higher.

It would not be surprising if 20% of the assisted suicide deaths are not reported.

Similar to the Oregon report, deaths from other illnesses have increased. The Washington State report indicated that deaths from other illnesses tripled. The report did not list what other illnesses but in Oregon other illnesses included diabetes.

The "safeguard" restricting lethal drugs to people who are terminally ill or within six months of death may not be followed. The annual report states that: 1 person in 2009, 1 person in 2010, 1 person in 2011 and 4 people in 2013 who received lethal drugs may be alive.

Further to that, when the status of the participant is unknown, the status of the lethal drugs is also unknown.

I am happy that these people may not have died by assisted suicide but I question how safe it is for people to have lethal prescriptions in their homes.

The Seattle weekly reported from the data in annual assisted report that there more woman than men died by assisted suicide. According to the annual report 96 (57%) of the assisted suicide deaths were women.

When Jeannette Hall asked for assisted suicide 15 year ago in Oregon but received supportive care, she choose to receive treatment for cancer rather than lethal drugs. Jeannette Hall is happy to be alive today.

When people receive good end-of-life care (physical, psychological and emotional care and support), they don't seek death by lethal drugs.

Assisted suicide is an abandonment of people. Assisted suicide is not necessary.
