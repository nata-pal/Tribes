This means it&#8217;s up to the prosecutor to prove that the accused is guilty. When it comes to drug offences, principles are put aside. In most Australian jurisdictions, we&#8217;ve got &#8216;deemed supply&#8217; laws. That means you can be charged with a drug supply offence based solely on the amount of drugs found in your possession. If you want to challenge the charge, you have to prove you weren&#8217;t going to supply the drugs to someone else. It&#8217;s an approach that should be abolished, according to a recent paper published in the journal Current Issues in Criminal Justice. So far, the only state to take this step is Queensland, which abolished deemed supply in 1986. This call to abolish deemed supply laws is a solid blow the respectability of drug detection dogs operations. These are allegedly targeted at detecting supply offences. They have never been good at doing that, and now it&#8217;s also clear that the deemed supply charges that they rely on are unjust.</p> <hr /> <h3><a href="http://www.unharm.org/time_s_up_tony_wood">Time&#8217;s up Tony Wood</a> [Unharm!]</h3> <p>Watching the show I was reminded again that it&#8217;s a tragedy that Tony and Angela Wood&#8217;s daughter died.
