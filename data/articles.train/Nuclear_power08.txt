formally begin until September, but the public already is being treated to extraordinary saber rattling and scare tactics from both sides of the controversy.<br /><br />President Obama and John Kerry warn that if the deal fails, Iran would win on both counts&#8212;they&#8217;d have trade and support from the U.N. and the other 5 countries, and they&#8217;d still develop a nuclear bomb, which would mean war.<br /><br />Some Republicans are so convinced that the deal that the U.S. and five other major powers negotiated with Iran to curb Tehran&#8217;s development of a nuclear weapon in return for sanction relief is so bad for this country and Israel that they are already entertaining fantasies of military action against Iran if the deal falls apart.<br /><br />Freshman Sen. Tom Cotton of Arkansas, an Army combat veteran of the Middle East conflict and arguably the single most vocal Republican opponent of the Iran nuclear deal, suggested this week to a group of Israeli journalists that the U.S. military has the capability of bombing Iran&#8217;s nuclear program &#8220;back to zero.&#8221;<br /><br />According to a report in the Times of Israel, Cotton argued that Secretary of State John Kerry and other administration officials had not been adamant enough in convincing Iran that the U.S. was willing to use force to prevent Iran from developing a nuclear warhead.<br /><br />&#8220;You can destroy facilities,&#8221; Cotton said, according to the report. &#8220;I don&#8217;t think any military expert in the United States or elsewhere would say the U.S. military is not capable to setting Iran&#8217;s nuclear facilities back to day zero. Can we eliminate it forever? No, because any advanced industrialized country can develop nuclear weapons in four to seven years, from zero.&#8221;<br /><br />Cotton, an articulate Harvard educated politician with real GOP star-power, fomented Republican opposition to the nuclear agreement with Iran months before the final version emerged from round the clock talks in Vienna last month.&nbsp; </b></span><br /> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b><a href="http://www.thefiscaltimes.com/2015/08/06/Iran-Nuclear-Dispute-Triggers-Saber-Rattling-Both-Sides">http://www.thefiscaltimes.com/2015/08/06/Iran-Nuclear-Dispute-Triggers-Saber-Rattling-Both-Sides</a>&nbsp;</b></span><br /> <br /> <br /> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b>Hastening War<br />By LEE SMITH<br /><br />War, President Obama says, is the only alternative to his deal with Iran. But if the president&#8217;s overriding goal is to avoid bloody conflict, why is he arming the Middle East for a shootout that may lead to Armageddon?<br /><br />The Iran nuclear deal lifts the U.N. arms embargo and ensures a huge cash windfall to Iran&#8217;s Revolutionary Guard Corps, which will fund its imperial wars across the Middle East. As a result, the other side is girding its loins for combat, too. Saudi Arabia is almost certain to go shopping for a nuclear weapon, now that the path is clear for Iran to get a nuke. But, of more immediate concern, the White House has been selling conventional weapons systems to the Sunni Arab states at record levels.<br /><br />It&#8217;s worth remembering that Obama believes these same Gulf Cooperation Council states&#8212;Saudi Arabia, Qatar, the United Arab Emirates, etc.&#8212;that have spent billions on U.S. weapons are also the most threatened from within. The Gulf Arabs&#8217; real strategic threat, Obama says, is not Iran but their own disenfranchised populations. In other words, the president is arming states he believes are fundamentally unstable, regimes that might not be long for this world. He wouldn&#8217;t give MANPADs to the Syrian rebels because shoulder-held missile systems might wind up in the wrong hands. Over the past several years the United States and other Western powers have engaged in an economic, clandestine and political war against Iran to force it to give up its nuclear program.<br /><br />Over the course of this siege, American policy makers have been very explicit about their goals. Foremost, to prevent Iran from becoming a nuclear power. Second, as John Kerry has said, to force it to dismantle a large part of its nuclear infrastructure. Third, to take away its power to enrich uranium.<br /><br />Fourth, as President Obama has said, to close the Fordo enrichment facility. Fifth, as the chief American negotiator, Wendy Sherman, recently testified, to force Iran to come clean on all past nuclear activities by the Iranian military. Sixth, to shut down Iran&#8217;s ballistic missile program. Seventh, to have &#8220;anywhere, anytime 24/7&#8221; access to any nuclear facilities Iran retains. Eighth, as Kerry put it, to not phase down sanctions until after Iran ends its nuclear bomb-making capabilities.<br /><br />As a report from the Foreign Policy Initiative exhaustively details, the U.S. has not fully achieved any of these objectives. The agreement delays but does not end Iran&#8217;s nuclear program. It legitimizes Iran&#8217;s status as a nuclear state. Iran will mothball some of its centrifuges, but it will not dismantle or close any of its nuclear facilities. Nuclear research and development will continue.<br /><br />Iran wins the right to enrich uranium. The agreement does not include &#8220;anywhere, anytime&#8221; inspections; some inspections would require a 24-day waiting period, giving the Iranians plenty of time to clean things up. After eight years, all restrictions on ballistic missiles are lifted. Sanctions are lifted once Iran has taken its initial actions.</b></span><br /> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/08/07/opinion/david-brooks-3-us-defeats-vietnam-iraq-and-now-iran.html?_r=0">http://www.nytimes.com/2015/08/07/opinion/david-brooks-3-us-defeats-vietnam-iraq-and-now-iran.html?_r=0</a></b></span><br /> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b><br /></b></span> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b><br /></b></span> <span style="font-size: x-large;"><b>Obama and the Flippancy of Fools<br />By Ed Lasky<br /><br />Over the last 7 years of the Obama era, Barack Obama and his team have regularly responded to serious questions with flippant responses. Don&#8217;t Americans deserve better than the flippancy Obama and his top officials dish out to us?<br /><br />The below compendium is just a sample (or a smidgen in Obama terminology).<br /><br />When asked why his trillion-dollar stimulus produced far fewer &#8220;shovel ready jobs&#8221; than he promised to urgently promote its passage, Barack Obama laughed off the issue: &#8220;Shovel-ready was not as shovel-ready as we expected.&#8221;&nbsp; The laughter will be little solace for taxpayers on the hook to pay back the debt that financed that union-enriching boondoggle.<br /><br />When asked about the risk of terrorism arising from his abandoning Iraq, he flippantly dismissed these concerns by comparing the resurgent Al Qaeda to a &#8220;Jayvee team&#8221; that &#8220;puts on Lakers uniforms&#8230; that doesn&#8217;t make them Kobe Bryant.&#8221; His flippancy -- his lack of concern and interest in dealing with Islamic terrorism -- has led to the rise of what has become the richest terror group in the world.<br /><br />He routinely shows the same insouciance whenever there are murders perpetrated by Muslims. When a man claiming allegiance to ISIS killed four Jews in a deli in Paris in January 2015, Obama dismissed the deliberate targeting of Jews since he said the attack was someone who just &#8220;randomly&#8221; shot &#8220;a bunch of folks in a deli in Paris.&#8221; The facts: the deli was Jewish, all the victims were Jewish (non-Jewish customers and employees were spared) and the murderer had said he was targeting Jews. They weren&#8217;t a bunch of random folks in a deli.<br /><br />Barack Obama could not bring himself to join other world leaders to protest the Charlie Hebdo murders in Paris. Indeed, it might actually accelerate it should Iran walk away from the deal after receiving its financial windfall. Even if Iran abides by its commitments, it provides Tehran with a massive program that could produce not simply a bomb but rather an arsenal. Nor does the agreement impose any serious inspections regime on the Islamic Republic. It falls short of requiring immediate ratification of the Additional Protocol, for example, so at best what Kerry negotiated was not the world&#8217;s toughest inspection regime, but rather the 125th toughest regime, as 124 countries have ratified the Additional Protocol already.<br /><br />Unable to rise above partisanship, he blamed much of Iran&#8217;s nuclear expansion on his predecessor rather than his adversary. Iran did not expand its centrifuge capability because George W. Bush had taken a more confrontational stance against the Islamic Republic&#8217;s nuclear ambitions. Rather, the reason why Iran achieved so much progress was because the European Union decided to increase trade, providing Iran with a hard currency windfall at the same time that the price of oil was also on the rise. Hassan Rouhani, at the time Supreme National Security Council secretary, invested that hard currency windfall almost exclusively in a cover nuclear program and ballistic missile development. When Iran&#8217;s covert nuclear enrichment plant at Natanz came to light, however, it is true that Bush neither embraced Tehran nor offered it $100 billion in sanctions relief. Instead, he helped usher through the United Nations Security Council a series of unanimous or near unanimous resolutions constraining Iran&#8217;s program rather than facilitating it.<br /><br />What was most disappointing in Obama&#8217;s speech, however, were his efforts both to stigmatize those who voted for military action against Saddam Hussein&#8217;s Iraq and to suggest his deal belonged in the same category as seminal Cold War treaties. First the Iraq canard: The world tried diplomacy with Saddam for decades before considering military options. Did Operation Iraqi Freedom go as smoothly as planned? No. Largely because Iran chose &#8212; in violation of the pledge of Kerry&#8217;s interlocutor Mohammad Javad Zarif &#8212; to smuggle in militias and explosively-formed projectiles.<br /><br />What Obama also doesn&#8217;t understand &#8212; and this applied both to Iraq and the Reagan-era negotiations with the Soviet Union &#8212; is that a core component of US strategy was defending and promoting liberty and freedom. I address this in more detail over at Commentary, but a crucial component of the Iraq war was promotion of democracy. Many can and do argue that this was naive and undercut stability, but the very fact that a debate between neoconservatives and realists dominated Bush administration policymaking is acknowledgement that democratization and transformative diplomacy had become core concerns contributing to policy.<br /><br />As for the Cold War, Democrats and Republicans both understood the danger which Soviet totalitarianism posed to the free world. Not only Eisenhower and Reagan, but also Kennedy and Carter stood up to Soviet aggression. Take Reagan&#8217;s call for Mikhail Gorbachev to tear down the Berlin Wall. No US president gave up the cause of freedom in the Soviet bloc. But that&#8217;s just what Obama has done with regard to Iran. Obama argued that those chanting &#8220;Death to America&#8221; don&#8217;t represent most Iranians, and yet in 2009 he largely stood silent in the face of mass protests.
